How does your business come across
in your customers' language?

When a traveler asks "where can I do a tea ceremony in Kyoto?", the answer increasingly comes from AI rather than Google — usually as a list of three to five places. Who gets in, and who doesn't? I test this myself, in Japanese, English, and Chinese, and keep the records.

From the July 2026 survey

I asked AI about ten cultural-experience businesses in Kyoto, in Japanese, English, Traditional and Simplified Chinese. A shop with over 500 positive reviews was said to "not exist", and was then described as a different business entirely — while the one business whose prices AI quoted accurately was a small, owner-run tea room.

Before the survey, I assumed size and reputation would decide the outcome. They didn't.

About this lab

Kamogawa AI Search Lab is an independent research project that measures how generative AI recognizes and describes businesses, and publishes the records.

This field goes by several names — GEO (Generative Engine Optimization), LLMO, AIO — and is new enough that none of them has settled. Which is exactly why I'd rather measure first and make claims later.

It is run by one person: an IT consultant living in Kyoto, working on this alongside a day job. Not affiliated with any university or organization.

What we can and cannot say

I can't tell you that AI optimization will increase your bookings. No one has demonstrated that causal link yet, and my own predictions have been wrong more than once.

What I can say is that change is measurable. Mention rate, AI-referred traffic, actual bookings — compare them before and after, and at least you know whether anything moved.

This lab sells nothing. I publish what I measure, including the hypotheses that turned out to be wrong.
